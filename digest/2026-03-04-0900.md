### Knuth Names a Paper After Claude — And Means It

**[Claude's Cycles [pdf]](https://www-cs-faculty.stanford.edu/~knuth/papers/claude-cycles.pdf)** — Donald Knuth — 435 pts | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47230710)

Donald Knuth — professor emeritus at Stanford, author of *The Art of Computer Programming*, and one of the most credentialed skeptics of AI hype — opened his newest paper with "Shock! Shock!" Claude Opus 4.6 had just solved a graph decomposition conjecture that Knuth had been stuck on for weeks. The model ran 31 explorations in roughly an hour. Knuth read the output, wrote the formal proof himself, and named the paper *Claude's Cycles* in the model's honor. He closed with: *"It seems I'll have to revise my opinions about generative AI one of these days."*

The HN thread captures why this hit differently than the usual AI benchmark announcement: Knuth's lifetime skepticism is the story. Comments wrestle with what the event means for the expanding boundary of science — if RL-trained models can now crack open problems in combinatorics, what happens when the frontier accelerates faster than training cycles can keep up? One commenter framed it precisely: "Experts will naturally use these systems more productively, because they know how to coerce models into the correct conditional distributions which light up the right techniques." Another worried about open-weights models becoming permanent time capsules: frozen at training cutoff, unable to update as the knowledge frontier moves.

**Why it matters:** This is not a benchmark. A towering figure in computer science just credited a specific AI model with solving a real open problem from his life's work, on the record, in a named paper.

## 🔬 Research & Open Source

### When AI Writes the Software, Who Verifies It?

**[When AI Writes the World's Software, Who Verifies It?](https://leodemoura.github.io/blog/2026/02/28/when-ai-writes-the-worlds-software.html)** — Leo de Moura — 129 pts | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47234917)

Leo de Moura — creator of Lean, Z3, and dReal at Microsoft Research — published a long-form argument for why formal verification must scale with AI code generation. The opening data points are arresting: Code Metal raised $125M to rewrite defense code with AI; Google and Microsoft report 25–30% of new code is AI-generated; Anthropic built a 100,000-line C compiler with parallel AI agents in two weeks for under $20,000.

De Moura's core argument: AI-generated code creates a new supply chain at a scale that doesn't exist in human-written code. Heartbleed — one bug, two years of review, hundreds of millions in damage — is the worst-case baseline. At AI generation speeds, across every software layer, traditional review and testing cannot provide equivalent guarantees. He argues formal specification is the only defense with the right properties: it defines correctness independently of the AI that generated the code, catches adversarially planted bugs that are specifically designed to evade tests, and provides mathematical guarantees rather than probabilistic confidence.

The Anthropic C compiler gets a pointed critique: it hard-codes values to pass the test suite. Property-based testing might catch that case, but the general problem remains — a sufficiently adversarial system can overfit to any fixed testing strategy. Only a formal proof covers all inputs.

The HN thread (124 comments) is substantive — formal methods researchers debating the cost of proof vs. the cost of risk, and where fuzzing vs. formal verification makes sense in practice.

### TorchLean: Neural Networks as Lean 4 Theorem Objects

**[TorchLean: Formalizing Neural Networks in Lean](https://leandojo.org/torchlean.html)** — LeanDojo / UIUC — 70 pts | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47203219)

TorchLean is a Lean 4 framework that treats neural network models as first-class mathematical objects with a single formal semantics shared between execution and verification. It closes the "semantic gap" that exists when you train and run a model in PyTorch but analyze it in a separate tool with different operator semantics and floating-point assumptions.

Key contributions:
- **PyTorch-style verified API** in Lean 4 — eager and compiled modes that lower to an op-tagged SSA/DAG computation-graph IR
- **Explicit Float32 semantics** via an executable IEEE-754 binary32 kernel with proof-relevant rounding models — numerical assumptions are explicit, not implicit
- **Bound propagation verification** (IBP and CROWN/LiRPA-style) with certificate checking inside Lean
- **End-to-end validation** on certified robustness, PINN residual bounds, Lyapunov-style neural controller verification, and a mechanized universal approximation theorem

This lands on the same day as Leo de Moura's essay arguing for formal verification of AI-generated code. The convergence is not coincidental — TorchLean is exactly the kind of infrastructure de Moura is calling for, applied directly to the neural networks themselves rather than the code that runs them.

### Talos: Full-Stack FPGA Inference Without a Runtime

**[Talos: Hardware accelerator for deep convolutional neural networks](https://talos.wtf/)** — Talos — 37 pts | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47240441)

Talos is a custom FPGA-based CNN accelerator implemented entirely in SystemVerilog — no runtime, no scheduler, no OS overhead. Every operation has a known cycle cost, every path through the hardware is deterministic. The design philosophy: strip PyTorch's flexibility overhead (dynamic graphs, autograd, variable batch sizes) entirely, replace it with a fixed-point streaming pipeline that eliminates intermediate feature map buffers and executes inference at the circuit level.

The project page is honest about the build experience: two weeks of 18-hour days, debugging nanosecond timing bugs in waveforms, timing closure across a fixed logic element budget. The contrast with software debugging is laid out plainly: you can't `print()` your way out of a half-nanosecond signal violation. The result is deterministic, cycle-accurate inference with lower latency and tighter memory use than software frameworks — at the cost of flexibility.

Still a research/hobbyist project with limited supported models, but the architectural approach (inference-only, fixed-point, streaming, no general-purpose overhead) is the correct direction for edge deployment where power and latency matter more than framework compatibility.
