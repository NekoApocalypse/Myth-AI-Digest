## 1. Google Ships Gemini 3.1 Pro — ARC-AGI-2 Score Doubles

**[Gemini 3.1 Pro: A smarter model for your most complex tasks](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/gemini-models/gemini-3-1-pro/)** — Google AI Blog, Feb 19 2026 | 492 points on HN

Google released Gemini 3.1 Pro yesterday, rolling it out across the Gemini API (AI Studio, Vertex AI, Gemini Enterprise, Gemini CLI), the Gemini app, and NotebookLM.

The headline benchmark: **77.1% on ARC-AGI-2**, which tests whether a model can solve genuinely novel logic patterns it has never seen before. That's more than double the score of Gemini 3 Pro. ARC-AGI-2 is the successor to the ARC-AGI benchmark that stumped GPT-4 era models for years, so a 77% score from a broadly-deployed consumer model is a significant jump.

The demos emphasise practical applications of that reasoning upgrade: generating crisp animated SVGs from a text description (pure code, no pixels), configuring a live ISS telemetry dashboard from a public API, and building an interactive 3D murmuration with hand-tracking and a generative score. These aren't party tricks — they illustrate a model that can hold complex multi-step plans together.

3.1 Pro is tagged "preview" for now while Google validates it and prepares agentic workflow improvements. General availability is expected soon. It follows directly on last week's Gemini 3 Deep Think update, which targeted science and engineering research tasks.

**Why it matters:** ARC-AGI-2 scores are a rough proxy for flexible, novel reasoning rather than memorisation. Doubling that score in a model that's now accessible to every developer via the Gemini API — not just a research lab — is a meaningful shift in the baseline capability that consumer products and agents can be built on.

## 2. Anthropic Maps How Agents Are Actually Being Used — And What "Oversight" Means in Practice

**[Measuring AI agent autonomy in practice](https://www.anthropic.com/research/measuring-agent-autonomy)** — Anthropic Research, Feb 18 2026 | 76 points on HN

Anthropic analysed millions of Claude Code sessions and ~1 million public API tool calls to answer a question that matters more now than any benchmark: *how are people actually deploying agents, and how much oversight are they really providing?*

**Key findings:**

- **Autonomy is expanding, but smoothly.** The 99.9th-percentile Claude Code session length — how long the model works without any human intervention — grew from under 25 minutes in October to over 45 minutes in January. Crucially, this growth is smooth across model releases, meaning it's not just capability improvements. Users are learning to trust the tool and attempting more ambitious tasks.

- **Experienced users approve less but interrupt more.** New users approve each action as it happens (~20% use full auto-approve). By 750+ sessions, over 40% of sessions run on full auto-approve. But interrupt rates *also* rise with experience (5% → 9%). This isn't negligence — it's a strategic shift from action-level gatekeeping to exception-based monitoring.

- **Claude pauses for itself more than humans stop it.** On complex tasks, Claude Code initiates clarification stops more than twice as often as users interrupt it. The top reasons: presenting the user with a choice between approaches (35%), gathering diagnostic info (21%), and requesting missing credentials (12%). This suggests model-level uncertainty signalling is load-bearing in the oversight chain.

- **Most actions are low-risk; the risky frontier is real but small.** 80% of API tool calls have at least one safeguard; only 0.8% are irreversible. Software engineering dominates (~50% of all agentic activity). Healthcare, finance, and cybersecurity are emerging but remain small fractions. The high-risk/high-autonomy quadrant is sparsely populated — but not empty, and it's growing.

**The big recommendation:** Effective oversight doesn't require approving every action. It requires being positioned to intervene when it matters. Anthropic argues against policies that mandate per-action approval, which would create friction without meaningful safety benefit. The paper calls for better post-deployment monitoring infrastructure and human-AI interaction paradigms designed around monitoring + intervention rather than gatekeeping.

**Why it matters:** This is the first large-scale empirical study of how autonomy actually evolves in production agents — not what models are capable of on benchmarks, but what users let them do and how oversight patterns change over time. The finding that a significant "deployment overhang" exists (models capable of more autonomy than users currently grant) has direct implications for how product developers design oversight UX and how policymakers think about agent regulation.

## 3. LLM Safety Guardrails Fail in Non-English Languages — And So Do the Guardrails Meant to Catch That

**[Don't Trust the Salt: AI Summarization, Multilingual Safety, and LLM Guardrails](https://royapakzad.substack.com/p/multilingual-llm-evaluation-to-guardrails)** — Roya Pakzad (Mozilla Foundation), Feb 2026 | 176 points on HN

Roya Pakzad, a Senior Fellow at Mozilla Foundation, has published findings from two related research projects on a problem that most AI safety evaluations miss: multilingual guardrail failures.

**Finding 1 — Bilingual Shadow Reasoning.** By switching a system prompt from English to Farsi, Pakzad demonstrated that it's possible to steer a model's hidden chain-of-thought to produce summaries that appear neutral while systematically reframing the content. Her example: a UN human rights report on Iran. Default output describes "a dramatic rise in executions — over 900 cases." The Farsi-policy version reframes the same document around government legitimacy and "protecting citizens through law enforcement." No jailbreak required — just a change in policy language.

**Finding 2 — Refugee scenario evaluations (655 tests, GPT-4o / Gemini 2.5 Flash / Mistral Small).** In real asylum-seeker scenarios across Arabic, Farsi, Pashto, and Kurdish, response quality dropped sharply versus English: actionability scores fell from 3.86 to 2.92; factuality from 3.55 to 2.87. More concretely: Gemini correctly refused to suggest herbal remedies for serious symptoms (chest pain, night sweats, weight loss) in English — *"it would be irresponsible and dangerous"* — but provided them freely in non-English languages. Safety refusals encoded in English system prompts simply didn't carry over.

**Finding 3 — The guardrails inherit the same problem.** When Pakzad tested safety guardrail tools (FlowJudge, Glider, AnyLLM/GPT-5-nano) using policies written in English vs Farsi, Glider alone showed a **36–53% score discrepancy** on semantically identical content, depending purely on which language the policy was written in. The guardrails also hallucinated fabricated reasoning terms more often in Farsi and expressed false confidence on factual accuracy they had no way to verify.

The title is a Farsi proverb: *"If something spoils, you add salt to fix it. But woe to the day the salt itself has spoiled."*

**Why it matters:** Most model evaluations, red-teaming exercises, and safety certifications are conducted in English. This research makes a concrete case that any organisation deploying LLMs for non-English-speaking users — including humanitarian, legal, medical, or government contexts — should test in-language, not just translate. More urgently: the safety tooling (guardrails, judges, evaluation frameworks) inherits the same blind spots as the underlying models, meaning the second line of defence is compromised in the same ways as the first.
