## 🔥 Top Story: OpenAI Takes Pentagon Deal Anthropic Refused — And Then Defends Anthropic

Three concurrent threads on HN (combined 910 points) tell one story.

OpenAI published [its full contract with the Department of War](https://openai.com/index/our-agreement-with-the-department-of-war) after the Pentagon blacklisted Anthropic as a "supply chain risk" for refusing to sign. The critical technical delta: Anthropic sought explicit categorical prohibitions on mass surveillance and autonomous weapons. OpenAI's contract instead defers to "all lawful purposes, consistent with applicable law, operational requirements, and well-established safety and oversight protocols" — meaning restrictions are only as strong as the current administration's reading of the law.

Gary Marcus [calls the whole episode a scam](https://garymarcus.substack.com/p/the-whole-thing-was-scam): Altman had been secretly negotiating the Pentagon deal since Wednesday — before he publicly expressed support for Dario Amodei, and after OpenAI president Greg Brockman donated $25M to Trump's PAC. Marcus: *"It was all theatre. Dario never had a chance."*

OpenAI simultaneously [posted a tweet](https://twitter.com/OpenAI/status/2027846016423321831) saying Anthropic should not be designated a supply chain risk — after having taken the contract that displaced them. HN commenters were scathing: the contract's "lawful purposes" language gives the DoD carte blanche since the executive branch defines legality. Top comment from a former OpenAI employee: *"My reading is that the DoW can procure data on US citizens en masse from private companies and apply OpenAI's tools to surveil them at scale. If I'm right, this is abhorrent."*

OpenAI's stated technical safeguards: cloud-only deployment (no edge, so no autonomous weapons), OpenAI engineers in the loop, explicit contractual references to current surveillance laws that survive future law changes. Whether those protections are adequate is the crux of the debate.

**Why it matters:** This is the sharpest public test yet of whether frontier AI labs will impose independent safety constraints on government use, or defer entirely to legal frameworks. The answer has architectural implications — Anthropic's approach required contractual red lines enforced by the vendor; OpenAI's relies on legal compliance plus technical architecture.

## 🤖 Models: Qwen3.5 Medium — Frontier Performance, Local Hardware

**197 pts** | [VentureBeat](https://venturebeat.com/technology/alibabas-new-open-source-qwen3-5-medium-models-offer-sonnet-4-5-performance) | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47199781)

Alibaba's Qwen team released three new open-source models under Apache 2.0:

- **Qwen3.5-35B-A3B** — MoE, 35B total / 3B active per token, 256 experts (8 routed + 1 shared), 1M+ token context on a 32GB VRAM GPU. Benchmarks beat GPT-5-mini and Claude Sonnet 4.5 on knowledge and visual reasoning.
- **Qwen3.5-122B-A10B** — server-grade (80GB VRAM), narrows gap with top frontier models.
- **Qwen3.5-27B** — dense, 800K+ context.

The architecture fuses Gated Delta Networks with sparse MoE and ships with native "Thinking Mode" (internal `<think>` chain before responses). Near-lossless 4-bit weight quantization is a first-class design goal, not an afterthought. The 35B-A3B actually outperforms the much larger Qwen3-235B predecessor on benchmarks.

Hosted API: Qwen3.5-Flash at $0.10/$0.40 per 1M input/output tokens — roughly 35x cheaper than Claude Sonnet 4.5 ($3/$15).

**Takeaway:** The gap between open-weight local models and frontier closed models continues to close rapidly. A 1M-context, Sonnet 4.5-class model that runs on a single 32GB gaming GPU is a meaningful threshold.

## ⚡ Inference: Unsloth Dynamic 2.0 GGUFs — Better Quantization Across All Architectures

**197 pts** | [Unsloth](https://unsloth.ai/docs/basics/unsloth-dynamic-2.0-ggufs) | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47192505)

Unsloth's Dynamic 2.0 quantization ships with several meaningful advances:

- **Universal MoE + dense support**: Previously Dynamic quantization only worked for MoE architectures (DeepSeek-R1). Version 2.0 applies intelligent per-layer quantization to all model types.
- **Per-layer customization**: Rather than applying a single quantization scheme, Dynamic 2.0 selects the optimal quantization type for every individual layer. The combination differs per model.
- **Better calibration data**: A 1.5M+ token hand-curated chat dataset (not Wikipedia) used for calibration, fixing the known problem of instruct models being miscalibrated on text-only datasets.
- **KL Divergence as gold standard**: Unsloth argues (with citation) that perplexity is a misleading metric for quantization quality due to output token cancellation effects. KLD and Aider Polyglot are more honest.

Benchmarks show Unsloth Dynamic 4-bit GGUFs outperforming Google's own QAT (Quantization-Aware Training) versions of Gemma 3 27B at a smaller file size. The 3-bit Dynamic DeepSeek V3.1 GGUF scores 75.6% on Aider Polyglot, beating many full-precision SOTA models.

Works with llama.cpp, LM Studio, and most standard inference engines. Fresh Qwen3.5 benchmarks also added.

## 🛠 Agents: Context Mode — MCP Output Compression for Claude Code

**212 pts** | [mksg.lu](https://mksg.lu/blog/context-mode) | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47193064)

An MCP server that intercepts tool outputs before they enter Claude Code's context window, compressing them through sandboxed execution.

**The problem:** With 81+ tools active, 143K tokens (72% of a 200K context) are consumed by tool definitions alone before the first message. Then each tool call dumps raw data — a single Playwright snapshot is 56 KB, 20 GitHub issues are 59 KB, a 500-request access log is 45 KB. A session hits the wall in ~30 minutes.

**The solution:** Each tool call spawns an isolated subprocess. The subprocess runs code, captures only the summary stdout, and that enters context. The raw data never does.

**Measured results across 11 real-world scenarios:**
- Playwright snapshot: 56 KB → 299 B (99.5%)
- 20 GitHub issues: 59 KB → 1.1 KB (98%)
- 500-row CSV: 85 KB → 222 B (99.7%)
- Full session: 315 KB total → 5.4 KB (98.3%)
- Session time before context degradation: 30 min → 3 hours

The knowledge base component uses SQLite FTS5 with BM25 ranking and Porter stemming. `fetch_and_index` fetches URLs, converts to markdown, chunks, and indexes — the raw HTML never enters context.

A `PreToolUse` hook handles automatic routing; no workflow changes required.

Open source, MIT: [github.com/mksglu/claude-context-mode](https://github.com/mksglu/claude-context-mode)

## 🔬 Methodology: VSDD — Verified Spec-Driven Development

**140 pts** | [GitHub Gist](https://gist.github.com/dollspace-gay/d8d3bc3ecf4188df049d7a4726bb2a00) | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47197595)

A detailed AI-native software engineering methodology combining Spec-Driven Development, TDD, and formal verification into a single multi-model pipeline.

**The core pipeline:**
1. **Spec Crystallization** — behavioral contracts, interface definitions, edge case catalogs, and crucially a *Verification Architecture* (which properties must be formally proven vs. tested, purity boundary map, formal verification tool selection).
2. **Test-First Implementation** — strict TDD enforced via prompting: "Write tests FIRST. Do NOT write implementation code until I confirm all tests fail." AI models naturally want to write both simultaneously; explicit constraint prevents this.
3. **Adversarial Refinement** — a separate model ("Sarcasmotron") with fresh context and zero politeness filters reviews spec, tests, and implementation looking for flaws. Context reset on every pass prevents relationship drift.
4. **Formal Hardening** — Kani (Rust), CBMC (C/C++), Dafny, TLA+ etc. run against the pure core. Designed-in purity boundaries from Phase 1 make this tractable.
5. **Convergence** — done when the adversary is forced to hallucinate flaws across all four dimensions (spec, tests, implementation, formal proofs).

**The key architectural insight:** Formal verification constraints must be decided in Phase 1, not Phase 5. A function that reads from a DB, calculates, and logs cannot be formally verified — it must be split into a pure core and effectful shell at design time.

**Multi-model orchestration:** Builder (Claude or similar, large context for code gen) + Adversary (different model family, e.g. Gemini, to avoid shared blind spots) + Human (strategic authority, not bottleneck).

## 🧭 Perspective: The 747 Problem — Coding Agents and the Erosion of Skill Accumulation

**120 pts** | [carlkolon.com](https://carlkolon.com/2026/02/27/engineering-747-coding-agents/) | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47182986)

A thoughtful essay from a programmer at an AI lab wrestling with a specific consequence of coding agents: the loss of incidental learning.

The core observation: when you solve a problem manually — reading docs, hitting errors, iterating — you accumulate knowledge as a byproduct. You're faster the second time. With coding agents, you skip straight to the solution, never learning the path. If you do it again, you're no faster.

The 747 pilot framing (from an actual conversation with a Belgian pilot): *"In this job, after a while, there's no improvement. You are no better today than you were yesterday."* The pilot envied engineers who learn as part of the job. The author now feels the same way about his own work.

The author's prescription: write some code by hand as a deliberate educational exercise, not a productive one. Try your own solution before asking the LLM. The domain knowledge that makes you a good prompter of agents still requires traditional acquisition.

Not anti-AI (he works at an AI lab, uses agents heavily). The essay is about the mechanism by which skill accumulation breaks down, and what to do about it.

The bans happened silently, without recourse, notification, or appeal.

The substance is platform governance: Google's systems are flagging and permanently banning developer accounts on automated signals with no human review and no appeal path. The discussion is primarily a community demand for:
- Warnings before permanent bans
- An appeal process
- Human review for edge cases
- Email/UI notification instead of silent termination

Limited technical substance on AI architecture but significant signal for AI tooling ecosystem: developers are increasingly dependent on AI CLI tools, and automated ban systems without recourse create existential risk to development workflows. Noteworthy that the trigger was a harmless import of a Python joke package.
